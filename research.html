Research & Design Reasoning

This document details the research thinking, assumptions, constraints, and evaluative reasoning behind the Latt project. It complements the main case study by focusing on *why* decisions were made, not *what* was built.

Research Question

How can users maintain awareness and control over recurring digital subscriptions without increasing cognitive load or compromising trust during sensitive financial moments?

Scope & Method

This project used analytical and comparative research methods rather than primary user interviews. Existing subscription products were examined to identify recurring friction points, failure patterns, and trust breakdowns.

The goal was not to measure frequency, but to understand mechanisms: how and why users lose awareness and confidence over time.

Key Assumptions & Unknowns

Assumptions

  • Users forget subscriptions primarily due to cognitive overload, not negligence
  • Recurring payments create passive financial loss over time
  • Trust is fragile in financial products, especially during onboarding
  • Users prefer familiar interaction patterns over novel ones

Open Questions

  • What level of transparency is sufficient to build trust?
  • How much subscription detail is helpful before it becomes overwhelming?
  • Will users allow limited email scanning if value is shown first?

User Mental Model

Users do not think of subscriptions as active commitments. Instead, they experience them as background processes that fade from attention once initial value is obtained.

  • Primary Goal: Know what I’m paying for
  • Constraint: Limited time and attention
  • Failure Mode: Forget → passive loss
  • Recovery Need: Visibility + easy exit

Trust, Privacy & Ethics

Financial data access is a high-anxiety interaction. Users are skeptical of applications that request permissions before demonstrating clear value.

Design Position: Trust must be earned progressively, not demanded upfront.
  • Value explanation before permission requests
  • Clear justification for each data access
  • User control over when and how data is used
  • No hidden background processes

Information Architecture Rationale

The primary interface emphasizes subscription visibility rather than features. Users should immediately understand their financial exposure without navigation.

  • Dashboard prioritizes active subscriptions
  • Alerts are contextual, not constant
  • Discovery is secondary to management

Interaction Principles

  • Familiar patterns reduce cognitive effort
  • Destructive actions must be visually distinct
  • No irreversible actions without confirmation
  • Errors must be explainable and recoverable

Alternative Approaches Considered

Full Bank Integration

Rejected due to high trust barriers and fear of misuse.

Fully Manual Subscription Entry

Rejected because it relies on memory — the same failure point the product aims to address.

Evaluation Plan

The design would be evaluated through usability testing focused on:

  • Time to identify active subscriptions
  • Clarity during failure and error states
  • User confidence during cancellation
  • Error recovery without external help

These assumptions were treated as hypotheses and directly informed interface prioritization, failure handling, and onboarding flow design.

Key Learnings

This project reinforced that trust and clarity matter most when systems fail. Designing for uncertainty leads to calmer, more reliable user experiences.
