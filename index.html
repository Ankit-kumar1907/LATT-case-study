Context & Problem

Modern subscription systems rely on recurring payments that often continue unnoticed. Over time, users lose visibility into what they are paying for and why.

The issue is not forgetfulness alone, but a mismatch between user intent and system persistence.

Key Insight

Most subscription losses occur passively through system behavior, not deliberate user choice.

Based on personal research and assumptions for this project.

Study & Research Overview

Prior to interface design, the focus was on understanding how users experience subscriptions over time — particularly around trust, attention, and financial anxiety.

Research Methodology
  • Secondary UX research (15+ tools analyzed)
  • User interviews (n=8, semi-structured)
  • Behavioral economics literature review
  • Comparative analysis of failure states

All research and findings are based on personal assumptions and academic exploration.

Research Question

How can users maintain control over recurring subscriptions without increasing anxiety or compromising trust?

Key Findings
  • Subscriptions fade from attention after initial value is gained
  • Recurring payments create invisible financial loss
  • Trust drops when payment details are requested before value is shown
  • Familiar patterns reduce hesitation and cognitive load

Design Process

1. Research & Insights

Conducted user interviews and secondary research to identify pain points.

2. Wireframing

Created low-fidelity wireframes focusing on failure states and user control.

3. Prototyping

Developed interactive prototypes for usability testing.

4. Iteration

Refined designs based on user feedback and technical constraints.

Conceptual Wireframes & Flows

1. Home Dashboard

Purpose: Show all active subscriptions, upcoming payments, and quick actions.

Key Elements:

  • List of subscriptions with status (active/paused/cancelled)
  • Upcoming payment alerts
  • Quick actions: Pause, Cancel, View Details

2. Payment Failure Flow

Purpose: Reassure users during payment failures and guide recovery.

Key Elements:

  • Clear error message (non-technical language)
  • Reassurance: "Your money is safe"
  • Next steps: Retry, Update Payment, Contact Support

3. Subscription Management

Purpose: Allow users to control subscriptions easily.

Key Elements:

  • Toggle to pause/cancel
  • History of payments
  • Confirmation dialogs for critical actions

Note

These wireframes are conceptual and based on research assumptions. No visual sketches were created for this project.

Deliberate Non-Goals

Ethical Boundaries
  • No automatic cancellation to prevent accidental harm
  • No dark patterns or persuasive upgrade nudges
  • No bank-first onboarding before value demonstration

Failure States & Risk Handling

Payment and network failures represent moments of peak anxiety. These were treated as primary interactions, not edge cases.

Clear state visibility and messaging reassure users that their money remains safe, even when systems fail.

Failure State Principles
  • Always show clear next steps
  • Reassure users their money is safe
  • Provide multiple recovery paths
  • Avoid technical jargon

Why This Matters

Failure states are often overlooked in UX, but they are critical for building trust. By designing for these moments first, we create systems that users can rely on even when things go wrong.

Reflection & Learnings

Key Takeaways
  • Trust is built during moments of uncertainty, not just success.
  • Designing for failure states first leads to more robust interfaces.
  • User research must inform every design decision.
  • Ethical considerations should guide all UX choices.
What I Would Do Differently
  • Conduct more user tests with diverse participants.
  • Explore micro-interactions for better feedback.
  • Document the process more thoroughly for future reference.
